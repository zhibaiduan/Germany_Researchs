SAP's 5 Acquisitions and 10 Key Partnerships Over Two Years

All pointing toward one goal — rebuilding the enterprise AI control layer

2026-05-18 22 min read

Research Objectives

This timeline is not simply a record of who SAP bought or partnered with. It traces how SAP is shifting enterprise software competition from "feature suite" to a battle for control over data, process, and AI execution entry points.

01
Identify what gaps SAP is filling — WalkMe addresses adoption, SmartRecruiters plugs the HR entry point, Reltio covers master data, Dremio fills the open data layer, Prior Labs adds structured-data AI.
02
Distinguish real capabilities from ecosystem signals — Databricks, Snowflake, and Google are closer to data-layer capabilities; Microsoft, AWS, NVIDIA, OpenAI, Mistral, Cohere, and Palantir each fill gaps in entry points, cloud, models, compute, sovereignty, and migration.
03
Assess what this means for the market — The question is not "SAP expanded its product line again" but whether SAP can pull AI competition back to where it is strongest: business semantics, core processes, and enterprise data.

Key Conclusions

SAP's main thread is not chasing general-purpose AI trends. It is assembling enterprise business semantics, trusted data, process execution, and user entry points into an operating layer that AI can call upon. Five conclusions frame the full analysis:

C1
WalkMe fills the "bought the system but nobody uses it" gap — not the root cause of SAP's product complexity. DAP adds an observable, guided usage layer on top of complex software: workflow prompts, contextual help, task automation, and adoption analytics. It helps users complete processes more easily, but cannot automatically make the processes themselves simpler. Pragmatic value, especially for large European enterprises.
C2
SmartRecruiters strengthens the SuccessFactors recruitment entry point — it does not change SAP's data foundation. HCM excels at managing employees, but recruitment marketing, candidate experience, and AI-assisted screening are weak spots. This acquisition lets HR customers avoid stitching together an extra system. The real test is integration: unclear boundaries with the existing Recruiting module will create duplicate purchasing and roadmap confusion.
C3
Reltio is the most foundational move of this cycle — it solves the prerequisite for AI to trust data. Joule and AI agents entering procurement, finance, and supply chain must first know which customer, supplier, or product they are dealing with. Enterprise data is scattered across systems, with the same entity appearing under multiple IDs and names. Reltio's cloud-native MDM consolidates fragmented records into a golden record — without this layer, the more AI automates, the higher the business risk.
C4
Databricks, Snowflake, Dremio, and Google Cloud collectively answer: how does non-SAP data enter SAP's business semantic system? BDC Connect's zero-copy sharing approach lets SAP's business semantics enter customers' existing data platforms rather than requiring data migration first. The core risk: three data platforms coexisting without a clear architectural division makes "openness" feel like "more complexity."
C5
Prior Labs is a long-term AI moat bet; the other AI partnerships fill model options, sovereign compliance, compute infrastructure, and migration execution. Enterprise structured data — financials, orders, inventory — is SAP's most valuable asset, yet general-purpose LLMs are not optimized for tabular prediction. If Prior Labs' Tabular Foundation Models reach financial forecasting, supply-chain anomaly detection, and procurement risk, SAP gains a structural AI advantage. OpenAI, Mistral, Cohere add model breadth; NVIDIA delivers inference infrastructure; Palantir + Accenture handle the last mile of migration.

These five acquisitions fall into two categories: WalkMe and SmartRecruiters improve existing product experience and application coverage; Reltio, Dremio, and Prior Labs are closer to the AI foundation — determining whether Business Data Cloud and Joule can be built on trusted data and structured AI capabilities.

Acquiring WalkMe

Digital Adoption Platform

WalkMe addresses a problem SAP has long been unable to avoid: buying enterprise software does not mean employees will actually use it, or that complex processes will be executed according to standard procedures. After cloud migration, Business Suite upgrades, and the rollout of Joule and agent features, the biggest fear for customers is not "missing features" — it is new capabilities that remain stranded in training materials and keynote slides.

A Digital Adoption Platform adds an observable, guided usage layer on top of complex software: workflow prompts, contextual help, task automation, and adoption analytics. Think of it as "real-time navigation and an adoption dashboard for enterprise software" — telling employees what to do next while giving managers visibility into which processes are genuinely being used.

Assessment: A pragmatic deal, especially suited to large European enterprises with strict processes, high training costs, and heavy compliance pressure. But WalkMe solves adoption and change management — not the root cause of SAP's product complexity. It can help users complete a process more easily; it cannot automatically simplify the process itself.

Acquiring SmartRecruiters

Talent Acquisition SaaS

SmartRecruiters fills the entry-point gap in SuccessFactors. HCM suites excel at managing existing employees, but before those employees join the company, recruitment marketing, candidate experience, high-volume hiring, and AI-assisted screening often determine whether an enterprise can acquire talent fast enough.

It treats recruitment as a conversion funnel: from reaching candidates, pre-screening, and interview coordination through to offer — every step trackable and optimizable. This positions SuccessFactors not just as an "employee system" but as something that engages earlier in the talent pipeline.

Assessment: Clear but unsurprising — primarily product-line reinforcement. It will not change SAP's data foundation the way Reltio or Dremio will, but it lets HR customers avoid splicing in a separate recruiting system. The real test is integration: if boundaries with the existing Recruiting module remain unclear, customers will face duplicate purchasing, migration friction, and roadmap confusion.

Acquiring Reltio

Master Data Management

Reltio is the most foundational acquisition of this cycle. For Joule and AI agents to enter core workflows like procurement, finance, and supply chain, they first need to know which customer, which supplier, which product they are dealing with. The problem: enterprise data is scattered across SAP, CRM, on-premises systems, and industry platforms, with the same entity potentially existing under multiple IDs, names, and versions.

Reltio provides cloud-native master data management — using entity resolution, data quality, golden records, and cross-system governance to consolidate scattered records into business facts the entire enterprise can trust. For BDC, this is more critical than "connecting more data sources," because AI does not just need more data — it needs trusted context.

Assessment: The strategic direction is very sound. MDM is not a new concept, but it is the most underestimated prerequisite for enterprise AI. The risk lies not in the technology terminology but in deployment governance: who defines the authoritative source, who reviews conflicting data, who is accountable for incorrect merges — none of these are problems that resolve automatically once the acquisition closes.
What is MDM, and why does it matter for SAP?

MDM stands for Master Data Management. It governs the most fundamental, frequently reused objects in an enterprise: customers, suppliers, products, materials, organizations, employees, locations, and so on. Its goal is not to build reports, but to answer a more foundational question first: which data should the enterprise actually trust?

Large enterprises typically run ERP, CRM, e-commerce, supply chain, finance, HR, and on-premises systems simultaneously. The same customer or supplier may appear under different names, IDs, addresses, tax numbers, and relationships across systems. MDM deduplicates, merges, validates, enriches, and governs that data to produce a trustworthy golden record.

This matters for SAP because SAP wants Business Data Cloud, Joule, and AI agents to operate inside core business processes. Without unified master data, AI might treat the same supplier as multiple entities, apply incorrect customer balances to decisions, or make predictions based on outdated product information. In short: without trusted master data, the more AI automates, the higher the business risk.

Reltio's value is precisely here: helping SAP unify key business entities across both SAP and non-SAP systems, so that downstream analytics, predictions, process automation, and agent execution rest on more reliable data foundations.

How does Reltio actually do MDM?

Reltio's approach is not simply renaming fields to match. It builds an entity resolution and master data governance layer above existing systems — mapping fields from ERP, CRM, finance, and supply chain into a unified data model, for example mapping VendorName, Account Name, and Supplier Legal Name all to a single "supplier name" attribute.

It then standardizes and matches: normalizing casing, addresses, country codes, company suffixes, and tax ID formats, before combining signals — name, address, tax number, email domain, bank account, organizational relationships, and source system — to determine which records likely refer to the same real-world entity.

To control false positives, Reltio uses confidence scores, matching rules, and manual review queues: high-confidence matches auto-merge, medium-confidence go to a data steward for review, low-confidence are flagged as potentially related only. After merging, it retains a crosswalk of each record's source system and applies survivorship rules to determine which field value is most authoritative — tax numbers from finance, contacts from CRM, legal names from compliance sources.

Its effectiveness comes from combining algorithm, rules, and human governance. It handles duplication, field inconsistency, and naming chaos very well — but cannot fully eliminate business ambiguity automatically. Enterprises still need to define data owners, authoritative sources, review workflows, and exception rules.

Acquiring Dremio (pending)

Open Lakehouse

Dremio solves BDC's openness problem. SAP may have the deepest ERP semantics, but customers' real data world will not exist only inside SAP: CRM, data lakes, cloud warehouses, manufacturing systems, industry platforms, and third-party data all participate in business decisions.

Dremio's open lakehouse capability lets enterprises query, analyze, and govern distributed data without large-scale data copying and migration. Open table formats like Apache Iceberg reduce the risk of data being locked into a single platform and make BDC look more like an open data layer than another closed warehouse.

Assessment: High strategic value, but also the most likely to prompt customer questions: SAP already has data partnerships with Databricks, Snowflake, and Google Cloud — why acquire Dremio too? If SAP cannot clearly explain "who handles storage, who handles compute, who handles semantics, who handles governance," the open data strategy will feel more complex, not less.
2026.05
Expected Q2/Q3 close

Acquiring Prior Labs (pending)

Tabular Foundation Models

Prior Labs advances SAP's AI narrative beyond "a business assistant that can chat" to a more fundamental question: the most valuable enterprise data is not text — it is structured tables: financials, orders, inventory, procurement, headcount. General-purpose LLMs can interpret text, but they are not optimized for making predictions, surfacing anomalies, or generating decision recommendations directly from tabular data.

Tabular Foundation Models can be understood as "a foundation model purpose-built for enterprise tables." Prior Labs' TabPFN approach targets classification, regression, anomaly detection, and few-shot learning — the goal being accurate judgments on new tabular data without large-scale custom training. In the SAP context, this means ERP could not just record the past, but actively support predicting the future.

Assessment: The largest upside — and the most in need of validation. The research capability maps well to SAP's structured data assets, but enterprise production environments will demand interpretability, permissions, audit trails, stability, and industry fit. This is a long-term AI moat bet, not a near-term revenue patch.

Key Partnership Timeline

The partnerships function more like SAP's external leverage: some fill product capabilities, some fill market entry points, some reinforce compliance narratives, and some exist to make customer migration actually happen.

2025.02
BDC launch

Partnership with Databricks

Data Intelligence Platform · Very Strong

Databricks was one of the most important endorsements at BDC's launch. SAP's narrative was no longer "move ERP data to the cloud" — it was getting that data into modern data engineering, machine learning, and AI analytics workflows. Without a data platform partner like Databricks, BDC would easily read as a SAP data warehouse upgrade.

The combination of SAP Business Data Cloud and SAP Databricks embeds lakehouse, AI/ML, and data engineering capabilities into BDC, letting SAP data and non-SAP data be used together within the same analytics and modeling environment. The key point for business readers: SAP wants its business semantics to enter the enterprise data platform — not remain confined to the ERP back-end.

Assessment: High value — one of BDC's most important external capability sources. But once Dremio and Snowflake join, SAP must clearly explain the boundaries between all three, or customers will interpret "open data strategy" as "yet more platforms to integrate."
2025.05
Cloud & model ecosystem

Partnership with AWS

Cloud & Generative AI · Very Strong

The AWS partnership is more about "making things run." SAP customers' cloud migrations and generative AI pilots need infrastructure, model services, joint customer scenarios, and implementation paths — and AWS has a ready-made entry in enterprise cloud and the Amazon Bedrock model ecosystem.

The focus of the AI Co-Innovation Program is not any single product, but jointly building solutions around SAP data, AWS infrastructure, and customer business scenarios. It serves sales, migration, and scenario co-creation rather than directly defining SAP's core AI differentiation.

Assessment: Medium-high value, but its nature needs to be clear — it is more of a go-to-market and cloud infrastructure partnership. Helpful for customer deployment, but its contribution to SAP's product moat is weaker than moves that directly touch the data layer, such as Databricks, Dremio, and Reltio.
2025.05
Office entry point

Partnership with Microsoft

Copilot & Teams · Very Strong

The Microsoft partnership answers the entry-point problem. Many business users spend their day in Outlook, Teams, Excel, and Copilot — not in SAP back-end interfaces. If Joule can only be invoked from within SAP, its usage frequency and business impact are inherently limited.

Through the SAP Business Suite Acceleration Program and integrations between Joule and Microsoft 365 Copilot, Teams, Azure AI, and BTP, SAP is trying to let users view business data, trigger processes, and invoke business actions from within familiar office environments.

Assessment: High value. Enterprise software competition is shifting from "whose system is most complete" to "who can appear where users work every day." But the boundary between Joule and Copilot must be clear — one owns SAP business semantics and process execution, the other owns office productivity — otherwise customers will not know which to ask, or which to use.
2025.05
Enterprise models

Partnership with Cohere

Enterprise Generative AI · Strong

Cohere represents SAP's multi-model strategy. Enterprise customers typically will not commit to a single model vendor, especially for knowledge retrieval, summarization, Q&A, and internal document processing — where security, deployment approach, retrieval quality, and cost all need to be weighed.

Integrated into SAP AI Core / Generative AI Hub, Cohere becomes an enterprise-grade model option SAP customers can call upon. RAG, embedding, and rerank — put simply — mean: first find the right material from the enterprise knowledge base, then have the model answer based on that material, ranking the most relevant content first.

Assessment: Medium-high value, especially for private knowledge and regulated industries. But it is not an SAP-exclusive capability and will not independently form a moat. True differentiation still comes from whether SAP can connect models to business objects, permissions, and process context.
2025.05/10
Zero-copy data

Partnership with Google Cloud

BigQuery & AI · Very Strong

The Google Cloud partnership further reinforces BDC's open posture. Many enterprises have already made BigQuery their analytics core. For SAP to get its business semantics into those enterprises' data workflows, it cannot require customers to first move all their data back into SAP.

SAP BDC Connect for Google BigQuery centers on bidirectional zero-copy data sharing — minimizing large-scale data replication and instead using permissions, metadata, and connectivity to let both sides use each other's data. Agent2Agent points at another question: can AI agents from different vendors eventually coordinate, rather than operating in parallel silos?

Assessment: Medium-high value, especially attractive to Google Cloud customers. It demonstrates that BDC is not a closed system — but its relevance depends on the customer's existing cloud ecosystem and will not be equally critical for all SAP customers.
2025.06
AI infrastructure

Partnership with NVIDIA

Inference & On-Prem AI · Very Strong

The NVIDIA partnership addresses a practical problem that arises once AI enters production: inference performance, cost, on-premises deployment, sovereign cloud, and large-scale operational capability. Demoing an agent is easy; getting it to run stably, compliantly, and under control inside a large enterprise is hard.

NVIDIA NIM, GPU infrastructure, on-premises Business AI, Joule agents, and physical AI scenarios all serve a single goal: enabling SAP's AI to run on higher-performance, more controllable infrastructure — particularly for manufacturing, public sector, and financial customers sensitive to deployment boundaries.

Assessment: Medium-high value, but it provides a runtime substrate — not SAP's core differentiation. What SAP still needs to prove is whether it possesses sufficiently deep business data, process semantics, and execution capability to produce distinctive business outcomes from that compute.
2025.09
German sovereign AI

Partnership with OpenAI

German Sovereign AI · Very Strong

In the German context, the OpenAI partnership is not simply "access to the strongest model" — it is about placing model capabilities inside a delivery framework that public-sector and regulated-industry customers can accept. German and European customers care not just about performance, but also about data residency, operational control, compliance accountability, and supply-chain trustworthiness.

OpenAI for Germany, combined with SAP Sovereign Cloud, aims to bring top-tier general-purpose model capabilities into environments that satisfy German and European data governance requirements. "Sovereign AI" here means not just that the model works — it also answers where the data lives, who operates it, who can access it, and how compliance responsibility is defined.

Assessment: Medium-high value, particularly important for German public-sector customers and the European sovereign AI narrative. But its applicability is narrower than the general enterprise market — more of a strategic signal and compliance delivery capability than the full source of SAP's universal product differentiation.
2025.11
Open data ecosystem

Partnership with Snowflake

AI Data Cloud · Very Strong

The Snowflake partnership lets SAP reach another important category of data customer. Many enterprise data teams have already built their cloud warehouses, permissions, sharing, and analytics workflows around Snowflake. If SAP only committed to Databricks, it would naturally lose a large segment of existing data platform users.

SAP Snowflake and BDC Connect for Snowflake similarly emphasize bidirectional zero-copy data sharing, connecting SAP's business semantics and the Snowflake data ecosystem with minimal data copying. This reflects customers' explicit demand: "stop creating another data silo."

Assessment: High value — helps ease customer concerns about SAP data being closed off. But it amplifies the positioning question: with Databricks, Snowflake, and Dremio all present, SAP needs to provide an architectural division of responsibility rather than just saying "all open."
2025.11
European model ecosystem

Partnership with Mistral AI

European Sovereign Model · Strong

The Mistral AI partnership fills the European model option. For German, French, and broader European customers, the geographic provenance and regulatory interpretability of the AI supply chain affect purchasing decisions — especially in public sector, financial, industrial, and critical infrastructure contexts.

Integrating Mistral models into SAP AI Core and sovereign-cloud-related scenarios lets SAP avoid dependence on American model companies alone, and builds a more complete localization and sovereign AI proposition for European markets.

Assessment: Medium-high value; strategic significance outweighs near-term functional differentiation. Model optionality alone is not sufficient — the key is whether Mistral can genuinely integrate with SAP's business workflows, permissions, audit trails, and industry semantics, rather than just appearing as another name in a dropdown.
2026.05
Migration execution

Partnership with Palantir + Accenture

AI Migration Tooling · Strong

The Palantir + Accenture partnership brings the conversation back to SAP's most realistic growth bottleneck: many customers do not fail to grasp the cloud ERP and AI vision — migration is simply too slow, too expensive, and too risky, with legacy system data, custom code, and organizational processes all entangled together.

The combination of Palantir AIP and Accenture's implementation capability aims to use AI to assist data migration, system analysis, and project execution — reducing the difficulty of S/4HANA and Cloud ERP migrations. Palantir's strength is complex data and workflows; Accenture's is large-enterprise implementation. The pairing is pragmatic.

Assessment: Medium-high to high value — it touches the problems customers will actually pay to solve: migration risk and implementation cost. But success will not be determined by tool demonstrations. It depends on stable delivery in complex customer environments, especially against hard problems like legacy customization, data quality, and organizational change management.

Overall Assessment

The core of SAP's moves in this cycle is not "buying more SaaS products." It is repackaging the assets hardest to replicate — business processes, master data, transaction data, industry semantics, and execution entry points — as the control layer for the AI era. Reltio, Dremio, and Prior Labs are bets closest to the foundation; WalkMe and SmartRecruiters solve adoption and application coverage respectively. The former determines whether SAP's AI can credibly understand the enterprise; the latter determines how quickly customers can see usable value.

Shortest path to value: WalkMe improves training and adoption management; SmartRecruiters strengthens the HCM recruitment entry point; Microsoft/Joule integration brings SAP actions into everyday office workflows.
Medium-term most critical: Reltio + Dremio + Databricks/Snowflake/Google — determining whether BDC becomes a genuine enterprise data layer, rather than simply SAP's own data export facility.
Longest-term upside: Prior Labs' Tabular Foundation Models. If they can reach financial forecasting, supply-chain anomaly detection, procurement risk, and workforce planning, SAP gains the opportunity to turn structured enterprise data into a distinctive AI moat.
Primary risk: Too many products, partnerships, and technical terms. Without deep integration into Business Suite, BDC, and Joule — and without clear architectural boundaries communicated to customers — this strategy risks being perceived as complex, expensive, and hard to deploy.
