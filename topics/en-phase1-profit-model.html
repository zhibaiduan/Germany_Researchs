Profit Structure, Customer Migration & AI/Data Strategy Transformation

How SAP is transitioning from a traditional ERP vendor to a cloud and AI/Data platform company

2026-05-14 18 min read

Research Purpose

This report addresses one central question:

From the perspectives of revenue, cost, profit, regional distribution, and customer migration: how is SAP transitioning from a traditional ERP vendor to a cloud and AI/Data platform company?

This question can be broken into four layers, corresponding to the four main sections of this report:

01
The Facts: Where does SAP's money come from, where does it go, where do profits originate, and which regions contribute most?
02
The Shift: Why are these revenue and profit structures changing? Is this SAP's deliberate strategic pivot, or pressure from customers, markets, and technology?
03
The Customer: Are customers willing to follow SAP's pace in migrating from legacy ERP / support models to cloud subscriptions?
04
The Verdict: Can SAP evolve beyond a cloud ERP company and become an enterprise AI + data platform?

SAP is not just a single software company; it is critical infrastructure within the German and European B2B software ecosystem. SAP's product direction, cloud migration strategy, AI and data platform positioning, and customer migration pace all influence consulting firms, system integrators, vertical SaaS providers, AI startups, data platforms, and enterprise IT budget allocations. Understanding SAP's profit structure and migration mechanics is foundational to assessing opportunities and risks in the German B2B software ecosystem.

Key Conclusions

The following five conclusions run throughout this report. They are distilled here upfront so readers can maintain orientation while working through the details:

C1
SAP's revenue center has shifted from license to cloud. In 2025, Cloud accounts for 57.1% of total revenue, while Software License has shrunk to just 2.7%. The primary revenue engine has moved from traditional license/support to Cloud ERP Suite-driven subscription revenue.
C2
Legacy support remains a cash cow, but is being gradually eroded by cloud transformation. Software Support still contributed 28.6% of revenue in 2025, but has declined from EUR 11.496B in 2023 to EUR 10.525B. SAP is using cloud growth to absorb the decline in support and license revenue.
C3
SAP's goal is not simply to "sell cloud"; it is to control enterprise process, data, and AI execution layers. SAP is combining Cloud ERP Suite, Business Data Cloud, BTP, and Joule / Business AI to attempt a transformation from ERP system vendor to enterprise process + data + AI execution platform.
C4
Customer migration is not frictionless; it is an ongoing negotiation. SAP is driving migration through RISE / GROW, legacy system maintenance deadlines, cloud-only innovation, and AI/data product bundling. However, DACH large enterprises remain concerned about migration costs, public cloud standardization, long-term contract costs, and vendor lock-in.
C5
Phase 1 cloud transformation is largely validated; Phase 2 AI/Data platformization remains to be proven. Cloud revenue, Cloud ERP Suite share, and Cloud gross margins all support the transformation narrative. However, the revenue contribution of AI/Data products has not yet been quantified separately, and whether customers are willing to hand over their future AI and data access points to SAP has not been fully demonstrated.

I. The Facts: Where Does SAP's Money Come From and Go?

This section answers only factual questions: what SAP's revenue, regional, cost, and profit structures look like. All data sourced from: SAP 2025 Integrated Report / Form 20-F.

1.2 Inside Cloud: Cloud ERP Suite Is the Core Growth Engine

Cloud revenue is not evenly distributed: 86.2% is highly concentrated in Cloud ERP Suite, while other Cloud products have even seen a slight decline.

Definition

Cloud ERP Suite is not simply equivalent to S/4HANA Cloud. In SAP's official disclosures, it includes SAP S/4HANA Cloud, SAP Business Technology Platform (BTP), as well as select HR/payroll, spend management, commerce, business process transformation, and working capital management solutions. More accurately: SAP's cloud growth is highly concentrated in the core business suite built around ERP, not dispersed growth across peripheral SaaS products.

Key Finding

EMEA has the largest total revenue (17,025), but legacy License+Support still accounts for 36%, the highest proportion among all three regions. Americas has the highest Cloud share (62.6%), with License+Support at only 25.3%, representing the most complete cloud transformation. APJ is the smallest in scale, but has the highest cloud growth rate (+72.5%).

EMEA retaining more legacy revenue is not because "European customers don't understand cloud"; it is because large enterprise SAP systems have been deeply customized over decades, tightly integrated with factory, warehouse, banking, tax, and supplier systems. Migrating directly to standardized public cloud can mean rewriting processes, interfaces, permissions, reports, and organizational workflows. Large enterprises more commonly opt for private cloud or hybrid migration paths.

Services gross margin of just 25.1% positions it more as a migration and adoption support function, not SAP's core profit engine.

Sales & Marketing at 24.1% of total revenue is the largest expense item; enterprise software selling costs remain heavy, with long enterprise sales cycles and high dependence on consultant relationships. R&D investment at 18.0% sustains ongoing Cloud / AI transformation, but absolute growth is modest, suggesting SAP is supplementing AI/data capabilities more through acquisitions (Reltio, Dremio, Prior Labs) than internal R&D.

II. The Shift: Why Are Revenue and Profit Structures Changing?

The facts above explained "what happened." This section answers "why": which changes stem from SAP's deliberate transformation, and which from customers and the market environment.

2.1 Internal Drivers: SAP Deliberately Redirected Growth Toward Cloud ERP Suite

SAP took three deliberate actions:

First, shifting the growth center from License to Cloud ERP Suite. License revenue declined from EUR 1.764B to EUR 0.990B, while Cloud ERP Suite grew from EUR 10.626B to EUR 18.119B. This is not organic change; SAP deliberately directed its sales, products, and customer migration toward a cloud-first model.

Second, designing migration pathways through RISE / GROW / Business Suite.

Pathway Target Segment Deployment Preference Positioning
RISE with SAP Existing large SAP on-prem customers Private cloud / complex transformations Migration pathway
GROW with SAP New customers, growing businesses, standardized processes Public cloud Migration pathway
SAP Business Suite All customers Cloud Target product destination

RISE and GROW are migration/sales pathways; Business Suite is the cloud product landscape SAP wants customers to ultimately inhabit.

Third, reorganizing the product portfolio around AI and Data. SAP launched Business Data Cloud and partnered with Databricks; completed the Reltio acquisition in 2026, and announced the acquisitions of Dremio and Prior Labs. The product portfolio has been reorganized into four layers:

Application Layer
Business Applications
Cloud ERP, SuccessFactors, Ariba, Concur, Commerce, Supply Chain, and other core business systems
Data Layer
Business Data Cloud
Reltio, Dremio, Databricks partnership, etc., connecting SAP and non-SAP data into a governed business data layer
Platform Layer
BTP
Handles integration, extension, automation, development, and AI access, the nervous system of the ecosystem
AI Layer
Joule & Business AI
AI agents that translate data and processes into recommendations, automated actions, and business execution

2.2 External Drivers: Customers, AI, and Capital Markets Are Also Forcing SAP to Change

SAP's external pressure is not an existential "imminent replacement" crisis. Its ERP is deeply embedded in large enterprise core processes, making switching costs extremely high. The real pressure is: if SAP does not bring customers onto cloud, data, and AI platforms, it will gradually lose control points in the future enterprise software landscape.

External Driver Magnitude Specific Impact on SAP
Legacy ERP customers entering upgrade cycles High Directly drives customer migration from legacy SAP Business Suite / ECC to S/4HANA and cloud; Business Suite 7 mainstream maintenance ends 2027, extended maintenance to end of 2030
AI requires cloud and data infrastructure High Determines whether SAP can maintain control of enterprise process and data access points in the AI era
Competitors eroding peripheral modules Medium-High Workday, Salesforce, ServiceNow, Microsoft, Databricks, Snowflake, etc. weakening SAP's control points in HR, CRM, workflow, data platform, and AI layer
Capital markets preference for predictable revenue Medium Reinforces SAP's drive toward cloud subscriptions; "more predictable revenue" share reached 86% in 2025, up from 75% in 2021
Overall Assessment

SAP's transformation is both a proactive search for new growth and a defense of core control points. In the short to medium term, the most direct pressure comes from the legacy ERP upgrade cycle; in the long term, the most critical pressure comes from the contest over data and process control in the AI era.

III. The Customer: Is Migration Going Smoothly?

SAP's cloud transition is not a one-way push; it is an ongoing negotiation with customers. Customers still acknowledge SAP's importance, but are unwilling to unconditionally follow SAP's migration pace.

Customers still recognize SAP as core infrastructure, but consistently raise four practical concerns:

Q1
After migrating to cloud, can existing customizations, interfaces, and industry processes be preserved?
Q2
Can the new AI/data products deliver demonstrable ROI?
Q3
Are the long-term costs of subscription contracts manageable?
Q4
Once inside SAP cloud, will customers' bargaining power and exit options weaken?

3.2 SAP's Migration Strategy: Incentives + Pressure

SAP's migration strategy can be summarized as "incentives + pressure": making cloud appear more valuable on one hand, while gradually increasing the costs and risks of staying on legacy systems on the other.

Incentives: Cloud as the Future Innovation Gateway
  • RISE with SAP reduces migration friction for large legacy on-prem customers moving to cloud
  • GROW with SAP offers new customers and standardized businesses a faster public cloud ERP onboarding path
  • Business Suite combines Cloud ERP, Business Data Cloud, BTP, and Business AI into a unified product narrative
  • New capabilities like Joule, Business AI, and Business Data Cloud are increasingly delivered exclusively within the cloud ecosystem
  • Acquisitions of LeanIX, WalkMe, Reltio, Dremio, and Prior Labs fill gaps in migration intelligence, user adoption, master data, data lakehouse, and structured AI capabilities
Pressure: Legacy Systems Becoming Increasingly Uncomfortable
  • SAP Business Suite 7 mainstream maintenance ends 2027; extended maintenance ends 2030
  • Product innovation focus is increasingly concentrated in cloud, Business Suite, Business AI, and Business Data Cloud
  • SAP's sales, contracts, and investor narrative all reinforce cloud subscriptions and predictable revenue
  • Consulting firms, system integrators, and implementation partners are increasingly reorganizing around RISE / GROW / Business Suite

SAP is not simply trying to convince customers they "like cloud"; it is reshaping customers' choice landscape: staying on legacy systems increasingly forecloses access to innovation, while migrating to SAP cloud grants entry to a new ecosystem of products, data, and AI.

3.3 Pricing Model: From License/Support to Cloud Subscription

SAP's fundamental commercial model shift is from one-time license + annual support to long-term cloud subscription + platform and AI/data add-on value.

Dimension Traditional Model Cloud Model
Core charge One-time software license Ongoing subscription payments (FUE basis)
Add-on charges Annual support / maintenance BTP, Business Data Cloud, AI agents, and other additional capabilities
For SAP License revenue volatile; support stable with high margins More predictable revenue, deeper customer lock-in, ongoing expansion potential
For customers One-time large upfront payment; subsequent support costs are transparent Long-term total cost uncertain; bargaining power may diminish; exit costs are higher

SAP's financial transformation is on track; its customer migration experience is not fully smooth. The revenue structure has changed, but customer mindsets and system architectures are still in transition.

IV. The Verdict: SAP's Position in the AI/Data Era

Evaluating SAP's transformation cannot stop at asking "has cloud revenue grown?" The more important question is whether SAP can evolve beyond a cloud ERP company to become an enterprise AI + data platform company.

4.1 What Does SAP Want to Control?

SAP's goal is to evolve from an ERP software vendor into an enterprise process + data + AI execution platform. This objective breaks down into four control points:

Control Point 01
Process Control
SAP is already deeply embedded in enterprise finance, procurement, supply chain, manufacturing, inventory, and HR core processes.
Control Point 02
Data Control
SAP aims to use Business Data Cloud to organize SAP and non-SAP data into a governed, comprehensible, and callable business data layer.
Control Point 03
Platform Control
SAP uses BTP to handle integration, extension, automation, and AI access, the technical hub of the ecosystem.
Control Point 04
AI Execution Control
SAP uses Joule, Business AI, and AI agents to advance AI from "answering questions" to "understanding business processes and executing actions."
Strategic Positioning

SAP's strategy is not to compete head-on with OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google in general-purpose foundation models. Instead, it is attempting to become the business layer that connects processes, data, permissions, and execution when enterprises deploy AI. What enterprises most lack is not the model itself; it is reliable business context, clean data, permission structures, process constraints, and executable access points. SAP already controls much of the core business processes and historical data of large enterprises.

4.2 Positive and Negative Signals

Positive Signals
  • Cloud transformation is financially validated: Cloud is now the largest revenue source
  • Cloud ERP Suite accounts for 86.2% of Cloud revenue, with cloud growth concentrated in core business suite
  • Software license decline has not undermined total revenue; cloud growth covers legacy model contraction
  • Cloud gross margin approximately 73.9%, entering a scaled, profitable phase
  • LeanIX, WalkMe, Reltio, Dremio, and Prior Labs acquisitions are filling critical capability gaps
  • Large enterprise customer base remains a moat: core ERP is not easily replaced quickly
Negative Signals
  • Customer migration experience still faces friction, particularly DACH large enterprises not fully accepting public cloud
  • Public cloud standardization creates tension with large enterprise complex customization requirements
  • AI/Data revenue contribution not yet clearly disclosed, still largely a next-phase growth assumption
  • Microsoft, Databricks, Snowflake, ServiceNow, Salesforce, Workday, and Oracle are all competing for the same enterprise data, workflow, and AI agent access points
  • The product portfolio is large; truly integrating Cloud ERP, BTP, Business Data Cloud, and Joule into a low-friction customer experience is highly complex

4.3 Overall Judgment

Phase 1 · High Probability of Success
Transition from license/support to cloud subscription
  • Financial data supports: Cloud at 57.1% of revenue
  • Cloud ERP Suite scale validated (86.2% concentration)
  • Cloud gross margin 73.9%, reaching maturity
  • Total revenue growing steadily (2023–2025 +17.9%)
Phase 2 · Direction Right, Awaiting Validation
From cloud ERP to enterprise AI + data platform
  • AI/Data revenue contribution not yet separately quantified
  • Whether customers are willing to hand AI and data access to SAP
  • Low-friction product integration experience still needs proof
  • Competitors all competing for the same access point

SAP's greatest competitive advantage in the AI era comes from its control of enterprise core processes and business data. Its greatest risk also comes from the same place: these processes and systems are so complex that customer migration is slow, standardization is difficult, and AI/data product deployment cycles are long.

The key to SAP's future competitiveness is not whether it can release more AI features, but whether it can genuinely combine Cloud ERP Suite, Business Data Cloud, BTP, and Joule into a unified platform that customers are willing to migrate to, willing to pay for, and that can deliver real business outcomes.
